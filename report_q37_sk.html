Zhrnutie

  • Margin squeeze intensifies: Slovak firms face a 3.7 percentage-point gap between expected non-labour input cost growth (8.37%) and selling price recovery (4.63%), nearly five times the EA’s 0.76 pp gap, compounded by labour costs rising for a net 62% of firms while turnover stagnated at a net 15% decline.
  • Bank financing eases, trade credit lags: The bank loan financing gap swung to -7.6 pp (need below availability), yet trade credit remained stressed with a +9.8 pp gap and a net 11.7% of firms expecting availability to deteriorate—highlighting a two-speed recovery in liquidity channels.
  • Cost pressures top concerns: Production/labour costs (6.19/10) and regulation (6.18/10) ranked as the most pressing problems for Slovak firms, with regulation’s pressingness rising notably by 1.18 points since wave 30, now matching EA levels.
  • 🔍 AI adoption gap: Slovak firms estimated only 25.5% of domestic peers would invest in AI over the next 12 months—6.4 pp below the EA’s 31.9%—while 46.5% reported experimental or infrequent AI use, 13.8 pp above the EA average.

Podmienky financovania

Slovak firms report net easing in interest rates, diverging from EA tightening

Changes in Terms and Conditions of Bank Financing (Q10)

  • A net 8% of Slovak firms (n=48) reported a decrease in the level of interest rates on bank financing — a reversal of 16.9 pp from the net easing of 25% in the previous wave — while the EA moved in the opposite direction, with a net 11% of firms reporting tightening (up 9.6 pp), highlighting a notable SK–EA divergence, suggesting Slovak firms may benefit from marginally lower borrowing costs compared to peers in the euro area.
  • Non-interest financing costs (charges, fees, commissions) tightened for a net 42% of Slovak firms (n=48), broadly in line with Germany's net 48% and well above the EA average of net 28%, suggesting Slovak firms face above-average non-rate cost pressures from banks — putting further strain on their financial margins.
  • The available size of loans or credit lines tightened moderately for a net 7% of Slovak firms (n=48), rising 14.9 pp from a net easing of 8% in the prior wave, converging toward — but remaining above — the EA net 4% and Germany's near-neutral net 2%, indicating a gradual but persistent reduction in credit accessibility for Slovak businesses.

Bank loan stress reversed sharply while trade credit gap remained elevated

Financing Need vs Availability Gap (Q5/Q9)

  • The Slovak bank loan gap swung from +10.5pp (wave 36) to -7.6pp (wave 37) — a reversal of 18.1pp — as need fell to a net 6.2pp of firms reporting decline (n=240) and availability turned positive at +1.4pp, leaving Slovak firms less stressed than the EA gap of +3.7pp.
  • By contrast, the trade credit gap eased only moderately from +16.4pp to +9.8pp (n=176), driven almost entirely by availability recovering from -6.7pp to +0.3pp while need held firm at +10.1pp, keeping Slovakia 3.0pp more stressed than the EA gap of +6.8pp — suggesting persistent liquidity constraints for Slovak firms.
  • The credit line gap also narrowed from +13.1pp to +4.8pp (n=256), as need moderated from +8.8pp to +3.1pp and availability improved from -4.3pp to -1.7pp, placing Slovak firms less stressed than the EA gap of +6.6pp.

Slovak firms' bank loan availability expectations improved markedly, narrowing EA gap

Expected Availability of External Financing (Q23)

  • A net 1.9% of Slovak firms (n=125) expected bank loan availability to deteriorate in the next quarter, a sharp improvement of 10.7 pp from the prior wave's net 12.6% — the largest wave-over-wave swing across all three instruments for Slovakia, implying a significant shift toward more optimistic expectations.
  • The SK–EA divergence on bank loan availability expectations narrowed to just 1.5 pp (SK: net 1.9% deterioration vs EA: net 0.5% deterioration, n=2,688), compared with a 13.4 pp gap in the previous wave, suggesting Slovak firms' pessimism on bank loan supply is converging toward the EA-wide view.
  • Trade credit remains the most stressed financing instrument for Slovak firms, with a net 11.7% (n=89) expecting availability to deteriorate — a 7.3 pp improvement from the prior wave's net 19.0%, yet still 11.2 pp worse than the EA average of net 0.5% deterioration, indicating persistent structural challenges in alternative financing.

Slovak firms sharply increased refinancing and product-development financing purposes

Purpose of Financing (Q6A)

  • 29.9% of Slovak firms (n=237) cited developing and launching new products or services as a financing purpose, rising 8.4 pp from the previous wave and outpacing the EA's 21.8% by 8.1 pp — the widest gap in at least three waves. • 20.6% of Slovak firms (n=236) cited refinancing or paying off obligations, rising 5.9 pp from 14.7% in the prior wave, compared with a decline from 17.0% to 14.3% at the EA level over the same period. • Inventory and working capital remained the dominant financing purpose for Slovak firms at 55.8% (n=237), rising 2.6 pp from the previous wave and exceeding the EA share of 38.8% by 17.0 pp.

Bank willingness to lend improved markedly for Slovak firms in wave 37

Factors Affecting Access to External Financing (Q11)

  • A net 10% of Slovak firms (n=300) reported that banks' willingness to provide credit improved, reversing from a net 2% reporting deterioration in the prior wave — a swing of 12.8 pp — and now exceeding the EA's net 5.7% positive reading, suggesting easier access to financing.
  • Slovak firms' perception of the general economic outlook remained sharply negative, with a net 38% (n=460) reporting deterioration, though this moderated by 12.9 pp from the prior wave's net 51% — still roughly double the EA's net 21% adverse reading, putting pressure on investment plans.
  • Access to public financial support continued to deteriorate for a net 25% of Slovak firms (n=328), easing 8.9 pp from the prior wave's net 34%, yet remaining well above the EA average of net 15% reporting deterioration — limiting a key buffer against tighter credit conditions.

Ekonomická situácia firiem

Slovak firms' profit contraction eased notably but costs rose further above EA

Business Situation Indicators (Q2)

  • Slovak firms reported a net 22% decline in profits (n=237), improving moderately by 15.1 pp from a net 37% decline in the previous quarter — yet still underperforming the EA's net 10% profit contraction, suggesting margins remain squeezed relative to peers.
  • Labour costs continued to press harder on Slovak firms than on EA peers, with a net 62% of Slovak firms (n=237) reporting rising labour costs — 11.6 pp above the EA's net 50% — and other costs accelerated sharply, rising 14.6 pp to a net 58%, outpacing the EA's net 51%, compressing profitability further.
  • Slovak firms' turnover remained negative at a net 15% decline (n=237), essentially unchanged from the previous quarter's net 15%, while the EA returned to modest positive territory at a net 6%, indicating weaker demand dynamics in Slovakia.

Slovak fixed investment expectations fell sharply, widening gap with euro area

Expected Changes in Turnover and Investment (Q26)

  • A net 11% of Slovak firms (n=169) expected fixed investment to increase over the coming quarters, declining sharply by 14.9 pp from a net 26% in the previous wave — more than ten times the EA's modest 1.3 pp easing to a net 13%, signalling a significant erosion in confidence among Slovak firms.
  • Slovak firms' turnover expectations remained broadly positive, with a net 21% (n=169) anticipating revenue growth, easing moderately by 3.3 pp from the prior wave's net 24% and converging toward the EA's net 23% (n=5,923).
  • German firms reported the weakest turnover outlook among the three economies, with a net 17% expecting an increase — marginally below Slovakia and well below the EA average — easing 0.9 pp from the prior wave, suggesting a relative underperformance in business sentiment.

Slovak firms expect input prices to rise nearly twice the EA rate

Price, Wage and Employment Expectations (Q31/Q34)

  • Slovak firms anticipated non-labour input prices rising 8.37% over the next 12 months (n=230), up 2.52 pp from the previous wave's 5.85% and widening the gap with the EA's 3.53% to 4.84 pp — the largest SK–EA divergence in at least four waves, implying heightened cost pressures for Slovak firms relative to peers.
  • Average selling price expectations rose to 4.63% (n=226) — up 1.22 pp from 3.41% in the prior wave — leaving Slovak firms facing a margin compression of roughly 3.7 pp between anticipated input cost growth and the selling prices they expect to recover, compared with a near-flat gap of 0.76 pp at the EA level, suggesting profitability risks if realized.
  • Slovak firms expected average wages to grow 5.49% (n=233), broadly stable versus 5.38% in the previous wave and running 2.38 pp above the EA's 3.11%, adding further pressure on operating costs alongside the sharp rise in non-labour input price expectations.

Slovak firms see stronger upside inflation risks than euro area peers

Inflation Risk Outlook (Q33)

  • A net 53% of Slovak firms (n=237) viewed five-year inflation risks as skewed to the upside, exceeding the euro area net balance of 45% by nearly 8 percentage points — the widest SK–EA gap in recent waves. • Slovak firms' upside inflation risk balance eased moderately by 7 pp from the prior wave's net 60%, yet remained above the euro area, suggesting persistent local price expectations despite some moderation. • Euro area firms moved in the opposite direction, with the net upside risk balance rising 5 pp from net 41% to net 45%, narrowing — but not closing — the gap with Slovakia's elevated reading.

Slovak firms rate costs and regulation as most pressing business problems

Most Pressing Business Problems (Q0B)

  • Costs of production or labour ranked as the most pressing problem among the seven tested for Slovak firms (n=228), scoring 6.19/10 in wave 38, marginally easing from 6.34/10 in wave 37 — yet remaining above the moderate threshold and broadly in line with the EA score of 6.48/10, implying sustained cost pressures that could squeeze margins.
  • Regulation rose to become the second most pressing problem for Slovak firms (n=225), scoring 6.18/10 in wave 38 — a notably higher trajectory than its 5.00/10 score in wave 30 (Δ +1.18 over the period) — and now closely matches the EA's 5.89/10, reversing a historically wide gap, suggesting regulatory compliance is increasingly burdensome.
  • Availability of skilled staff ranked third for Slovak firms (n=228) at 6.07/10, easing marginally from 6.57/10 in wave 37, while access to finance remained the least pressing of the seven problems tested at 3.91/10 — a low-priority score well below the moderate threshold, indicating limited immediate liquidity concerns despite broader labour market tightness.

Špeciálna téma: Digital transformation

Slovak firms show lower AI adoption intensity than the EA, with notably higher reliance on experimental use.

Special Focus: Digital transformation

  • Experimental AI use dominant: 46.5% of SK firms reported very infrequent or experimental AI use, compared to 32.7% in the EA — a difference of 13.8 pp — while moderate use was recorded by only 17.1% in SK versus 31.2% in the EA.
  • AI perceived as irrelevant: 31.7% of SK firms cited AI not being useful as a barrier to adoption, versus 26.1% in the EA; by contrast, lack of skills ranked lower in SK (25.7%) than EA (31.8%).
  • Lower domestic AI uptake expectations: Slovak firms estimated that 15.6% of similar domestic firms had invested in AI, broadly in line with the EA mean of 17.1%, but expected only 25.5% of domestic peers to invest over the next 12 months versus 31.9% in the EA.
  • Core-process use less common in SK: Among AI-using firms, only 11.7% of SK firms cited improving core area processes as a reason, compared to 21.3% in the EA, while non-core process improvement was the dominant motive in both (47.9% SK vs. 42.2% EA).
  • Planned AI investment share comparable: Both SK firms and EA firms reported a median of 5% of total investment allocated to AI over the next 12 months, with SK mean at 7.2% versus EA mean at 9.1%.
